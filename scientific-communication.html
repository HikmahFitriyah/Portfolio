Art for Scientific Communication

My work challenges conventional approaches to scientific communication that prioritise technical accuracy and didactic explanation. By experimenting with storytelling, cartoons, and visual metaphors, I investigate how art can communicate complex scientific ideas while fostering curiosity, engagement, and accessibility.

Investigating animated patient stories in enhancing the understanding of genetics and hereditary cancer syndromes

Led by: Assoc. Prof Joanne Ngeow and Assoc. Prof Davide Benvenuti

Lee Kong Chian School of Medicine, Nanyang Technological University

Cancer Genetics Service, Division of Medical Oncology, National Cancer Centre, Singapore

School of Art, Design, and Media, Nanyang Technological University, Singapore

Animation has been widely used for education in healthcare settings. However, many educational materials rely on didactic approaches, and research examining narrative-based animations remains limited. This study investigates the acceptability of visual storytelling in facilitating genetic education on hereditary cancer. Over four years, a multidisciplinary team—comprised of clinicians, researchers, and animators—developed a framework for co-designing and evaluating animated patient stories for patient education.


Our findings showed that animated patient narratives increased viewers’ interests and understanding of genetic concepts. Additionally, participants preferred animations that incorporated dramatised narratives, which enhanced both emotional and cognitive engagement. These findings highlight the potential of animated patient stories as an accessible educational tool, particularly in bridging literacy gaps in healthcare settings.

Balancing whimsical art and scientific communication

Scientific communication is often presented through diagrams and explanatory texts. While effective, these approaches can sometimes feel too technical for non-expert audiences. This ongoing project explores how whimsical illustrations, cartoons, and narrative storytelling can offer alternative ways of communicating scientific ideas.


Rather than presenting information purely through didactic explanation, this body of work aims to spark curiosity and interest. By using playful visual metaphors, audiesnces are encourages to engage with scientific ideas in a more intuitive and curious way. This project is an ongoing practice-based exploration, where different storytelling techniques and visual metaphors are tested to understand how artistic approaches can support science communication.
